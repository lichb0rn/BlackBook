# [[Logos]]

Many of the [[Stoicism|Stoics]] did not believe in anything like the modern monotheistic conception of God. Their preferred word for it was Logos, which can be interpreted as the Word of God (as the Christians who inherited a lot of Stoic philosophy did), or as a kind of Providence embedded in the very fabric of the universe, or even more simply as the rather straightforward observation that the cosmos can be understood rationally, regardless of how it came to be.

In this sense, the Stoics can be thought of as pantheistic (or perhaps panentheistic)‚Äîthat is, as believing that God is the universe itself and therefore we all partake in the divine nature. The only difference between human beings and other animals is that we are capable of the highest attribute of God/Universe: **reason**.

This identification of God with Nature has a long history, and it was developed particularly by the influential seventeenth-century Dutch philosopher [[Baruch Spinoza]].
